How it Worked

Observations

From the moment the customer thought about us to when we left their mind we tracked it. All digital interactions were marked.

It became clear what the customer thought we're responsible for. The rest was opportunity.

Emotions

We drew attention to the softer side of the experience by tracking frustration, joy, and more. Direct observation showed us how the customer was feeling before, after, and during the experience.

What wasn't working quickly came into focus for improvement.

Blinders

An analytics only view using our digital tracking tools was overlaid to understand the extent of what we could and couldn't see in person. All interactions in the flow not seen by analytics were marked off and blocked out.

We found we continued to miss the majority of the experience.

Measures

We also tracked what led up to a decision, what happened after it, and how a user reacted to create unique scenarios.

Indicators of what directly affects customer behavior, satisfaction, and efficiency became signals to watch increasing our certainty of a positive impact on the customer experience.

Small Gestures, Big Impact

Game Ready

Pillars of the experience was the framework used to understand impact. Showing a team exactly where they played before they decided how to win never was easier.

Technology Second

The needs of the customer had never been clearer allowing us to freely choose how to meet expectations. No longer was the current hardware a box we were forced to think in.

Cost Sensitive

From one field study alone we were able to identify the exact point within the ordering experience that was costing our customers over $10mm/yr from frustration and friction.

Fuller Perspective

This approach supported any team wanting to take an important deeper look. The focus was on witnessing how we influenced the life of the customer. We quickly found that what matters to our users was lacking attention, what didn’t was getting priority, and where opportunity in the industry existed was unclaimed. The customer had never gotten this level of attention.

Tomorrow's Reality

Take a peek into the future we see for independent grocery ordering.

The speed of sight. Customers hands are always half full, impairing their ability to use their full body to complete tasks. Our best tool, our eyes, will be the primary form of interaction.
Act and correct at will. Every ordering event is singular making issues with an item just as important as the purchase. Customers will to be able to engage with their shelves with large natural gestures rather than one scan at a time.
Distance and perspective. Building an order requires up close examination of a shelf - sometimes even on hands and knees. Whatever height, distance, and angle a customer feels they need to operate at, technology will rise to meet them.
Infinite canvas. Today's customer uses tiny screens to squint, tap, and shake their way to a completed task. Unrestricted by screen real estate they will have more space to operate than 100 screens combined.
Observe and reflect. Lack of object permanence with a multi-isle order leads to duplicates and missed items. Customers will soon know where they left off anywhere in their store allowing them to return at any point.

Click behavior and navigation paths

Rarely did we get to understand the physical circumstances influencing the parts of the website customers would engage with. For example, we never saw the small things like how much screen real estate was occupied by browser ads.

Privacy and security risks

Customers regularly posted on public sticky notes their personal logins, truck delivery windows, and other sensitive information because our service wasn’t appropriately storing and serving it to them.

Third party devices

We were completely unaware of competing peripherals that customers used for unrelated tasks that we could be leveraging to cut down the time it took to complete certain work.

Manual hand calculations

Modern tasks existed that customers still needed help completing requiring a calculator. These were things that we were not calculating for them directly in the browser because we were not aware it was needed.

Printing paper invoices

All invoices had a digital twin yet the in person needs of the customer required an physical invoice that could be brought with them and marked up.
